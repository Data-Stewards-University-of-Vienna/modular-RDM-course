# Data Organization

## Objectives
Learners will be able to:

* describe the relevance of file and folder naming conventions 
* state criteria for developing file and folder organization schemas
* develop naming conventions
* apply naming conventions to their own (and collaboratively) used research data
  
## Introduction
**The Challenge**
Data flows from raw data to results, via tables and figures to communication outlets. We know there will be LOTS of files, that they can change over time (versions), have relationships to each other, and will be required to be understandable as the basis of our findings and publications.
<img width="337" height="318" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/94b6f07a-7445-4876-93d9-7f4e5a5c2d77" /> (JW, 2026-06-17_RDM_CoBeNe_Presentation_in-person_v02.key)

**The Solution**
Just like in a well-sorted kitchen, it will make your work and collaboration a lot faster if it is clear where to find things. This means, a file name and location need to be very informative even before you open it. The more self-explanatory the better.

There are two angles to judge naming quality: Can you decide whether you want to open a folder or file, i.e., whether is it what you are looking for? Can you sort a file into a folder so you will able to find it again, i.e., do the folders match the natural categories of your work?

Organizing files makes them more findable and accessible (feeding into the FAIR principles) and enable "someone" to understand what you did - that someone usually is you in 6 months.

**The Imperative**
Plan and establish naming and structuring systems, document them, and use them consistently.

Any system is better than none!

A well-designed folder structure can help you quickly and easily locate the data you need, saving you time and effort. A clear and consistent folder structure can also help you collaborate effectively with other members of your research team. By establishing a standardized approach, everyone involved can easily access and understand your data.

Design clear folder structures that align with the project: Before starting your research, plan out a folder structure that aligns with the objectives and goals of your project. This structure should be intuitive, easy to understand, and should follow a logical sequence of stages.
Use the same structure for different locations: If you are working on the project across multiple computers or with different team members, ensure that the same folder structure is used across all locations.
Use max. 4 levels of folders: Keep the folder structure as simple as possible by limiting the number of levels to a maximum of four. This will make it easier to navigate and manage.
Classify folders according to different project stages (raw, processed, analyzed): Classify the folders based on the different stages of the project, such as raw data, processed data, and analyzed data. This will help you to organize the files based on their current stage of completion and make it easier to locate the data when needed.
Document the naming scheme and folder structure: Once you have finalized the folder structure, document it clearly to avoid confusion and ensure that all team members are on the same page.
